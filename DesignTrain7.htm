Week 7 of 12: Visual Hierarchy

Last week we learned about design principles, and analyzed their presence within a simple component of the Google+ interface. If you haven't yet, I encourage you to check out the comments. Hopefully it's starting to make sense how principles that make a design beautiful can also be used to meet the goals of a design.


Mastering the factors of visual hierarchy

When attempting to make good use of all of these design principles, it can be tempting to try lots of different fonts, colors, rule lines, and effects in a design. But when you do that, boy does it make it hard to keep your design looking right.

The problem is, each of these little things – the fonts, the colors, the ornamentations and effects – adds a ton of complexity to the visual design equation.

Before you can learn to run, you have to learn to walk. Before you can use a bunch of different colors, fonts, and effects in a design, you have to learn how to make a design work without any of those things. In fact, after learning this, you may find that you rarely need any of those things anyway.

Take, for example, this table. Almost any software program that makes tables will, by default, make tables a lot like this:
 


 


It makes sense. All of these rule lines are supposedly there to help guide your eye from one column or row to the next. They're supposedly there to separate the pieces of information from one another.

But you find that if you completely remove all of these rule lines, you have a table that is not only still functional, but overall has a more "clean" look.
 


 

It looks "clean" because there aren't a bunch of superfluous elements in the table. The whitespace between pieces of information and the imaginary lines upon which those pieces of information are organized are enough to guide your eye throughout the table. Notice, however, that the percentages on the table are italicized to differentiate that information.


Visual hierarchy for clean design

It's this type of precise thinking about visual elements that separates a design that looks "clean" from a design that looks like a mess. You have to master the individual factors for establishing a visual hierarchy, one at a time.

Here's a slightly more complex composition, with many different types of information. It's the title, metadata, and summary of a blog post.


 

Notice that it's all the same font, all the same size of type, and all the same color of type. The only factor that is differentiating information is the alignment of elements, and the white space between them. One little trick I've done is to put the title in all caps, and to letterspace those capitals a little bit (only do wide letterspacing like this with capitals, not with lowercase type).

In this next composition, I've added a couple more factors. I've picked various sizes of type (based upon the type scale I talked about in week 5), and am now working with various weights of type – I've thrown bold type into the mix. The body copy is arguably too long to be all caps, but hey, I'm working with strict requirements here.

Now I can get even more complex by adding the factors of color and ornamentation. Notice I didn't make the title big to make it pop out. I just made it red, and put it really close to the big black bar that you just can't ignore.



As I add factors, the possibilities increase exponentially. But is the last composition really that much more effective than the first? When you take into account how much tougher it is to take into account changes in weight, size, color, and ornamentation, the payoff is negligible.

I haven't even thrown another typeface into the mix, and this is an unrealistically simple composition. When you're designing complex applications, the possibilities increase yet again, and clarity will suffer if you don't use new factors wisely.
 

Keep it simple

So, next time you're designing something, and you feel tempted to add another font, another rule line, or even to make something bold or italic – stop. Take some time to play around with the spacing and alignment of things. How can you simplify your decisions and get your point across without a bunch of extra stuff?
 

Extra credit

The extra credit you'll get will of course come only in the form of your own learning, but if you'd like to play around with the above composition, here is a PDF template that you can open in Illustrator and play around with.

If you don't have Illustrator, you can use the program of your choice (Keynote, PowerPoint, Photoshop – whatever) and reconstruct the composition (the canvas has a 3:4 ratio). Just use Helvetica and one size and weight of type, and see how you can change the visual hierarchy just by moving things around. It may be helpful to give yourself a margin on the canvas and construct a grid.
